Knee

Anterior Drawer Test

Back

Patient Position

Lying supine with hip flexed at 45 degrees with knee flexed at 90 degrees.

Examiner Position

Sitting on patient's involved leg's foot on examination table, infront of affected knee.

Examiner Hand Position

One hand grasps the tibial joint line right behind the knee with thumbs placed along the joint line on both sides of the patellar tendon, while index fingers are along hamstring tendons to ensure they are relaxed.

Evaluation Procedure

Tibia is drawn anteriorly

Positive Test

Increased amount of anterior tibial translation when compared bilaterally; lack of a firm endpoint.

Implications

Sprain of the Anterior Cruciate Ligament.

Modifications/Comments

There may be a posterior sag of the knee due to previous Posterior Cruciate Ligament trauma; hamstrings must be relaxed to avoid false negative; this test is better when detecting older ACL injuries when compared to acute ACL injuries.

Evidence

  • Sensitivity: 60%
  • Specificity: 85%

Evidence Resource

Starkey C, Brown SD. Examination of Orthopedic & Athletic Injuries. 4th ed. Philadelphia: F.A. Davis Company; 2015.
